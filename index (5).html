About

The person behind the work.

Personal
Family Fitness Travel Cal Poly SLO SF Bay Area

I was born and raised in Menlo Park, to parents who were each one of nine kids. Independence has always been in my blood. Growing up, I was competitive before I knew what competition meant: playing basketball from age five, going to the championship in eighth grade, breaking the 4x100 relay record at our track championship, then carrying that intensity into field hockey and lacrosse in high school.

When I got to Cal Poly, I channeled that same drive into figuring out what actually sparked my curiosity. I found my people in the Financial Management Association and went to New York to visit leading investment banks, venture firms, and the NYSE. Banking didn't feel like me, but the one VC stop on that trip was something I couldn't stop thinking about. The technology. The strategy. The possibility of it.

That stop turned into a summer at Defy.vc, a $300M oversubscribed early stage fund, working directly with the Managing and Founding Partner across the full investment lifecycle. I wrote 10 investment memos spanning AI, infrastructure, logistics, construction tech, and CPG, each one built from a full competitive landscape and return model, not a template. One of them, on Aztra Labs, came together in 48 hours and ended up distributed firm wide and to co investors at partner firms.

Writing memo after memo, I kept hitting the same bottleneck: the research process was nearly identical every time, but I was rebuilding it from scratch. So I built an AI agent to handle the repeatable parts of diligence, sourcing comparables, structuring the first pass of a competitive landscape, so I could spend my time on the judgment calls that actually needed a person. It is the kind of problem I want to keep solving: not just doing the analysis, but building the system that makes the analysis better.

I found myself so obsessed with each company I was covering that I wanted to join every single one.
On writing venture memos at Defy.vc

Before that summer, I got my foot in the door at Shift 5, a defense tech startup, doing FP&A work that went directly to the CFO and building the financial systems a company needs before it can raise its next round. Between the two, I have sat on both sides of the table now, the fund deciding where to invest, and the company trying to earn that investment.

What I want next is to stop moving company to company and go deep on one. I want to be in the weeds, figuring out what makes a high growth company tick, and helping it capitalize on it.

Context

A quarter long valuation of UPS, a roughly $91B revenue global logistics company, from industry analysis to a defensible view on the stock.

What I did

  • Built historical and projected financials from 2020 to 2029.
  • Estimated a bottom up beta and ran both DCF and APV intrinsic valuations.
  • Benchmarked over 40 global logistics comparables with a multiples regression.
  • Stress tested the value across risk free rate, beta, cost of debt, and terminal growth.

Findings

The APV came within about 3.5% of market price. Sensitivity analysis showed terminal growth was the dominant driver across every scenario.

Context

A set of fixed income workshops applied to live instruments: US Treasuries, a $1B Apple bond maturing in 2043, and consumer credit products.

What I did

  • Bootstrapped a 10 year zero coupon spot curve.
  • Built Apple's issuer credit curve from its outstanding bonds.
  • Compared model value to market price to make buy or sell calls.
  • Modeled amortization and APR spreads against the prime and Treasury curves.

So what

Where intrinsic value diverged from the quoted price, I could name the mispricing and the trade. That is the core move behind any rates or credit decision.
